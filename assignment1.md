# Hearts of Iron IV Starter Guide

Hearts of Iron IV is a grand strategy game about leading a country through the years before and during the Second World War. At first it can look overwhelming: there are factories, armies, research slots, diplomacy, production lines, naval missions, air wings, and national focuses all demanding attention at once. The good news is that a beginner does not need to master every system immediately. A solid first game is about understanding the basic rhythm: build industry, research useful technology, produce equipment, organize an army, and choose limited goals.

This guide is written for a new player who wants to survive the early confusion and make meaningful decisions without pausing every ten seconds.

## 1. Choose a Beginner-Friendly Country

For a first campaign, choose a country that has enough resources to teach the main systems but is not instantly destroyed by a stronger neighbor. Germany is often recommended because it has a strong industry, clear goals, and an early war path. The United States is safer but slower, while the Soviet Union is powerful but more complicated. Avoid very small countries at first, because they give you fewer tools and punish mistakes more harshly.

A good first objective is not "win the entire war perfectly." Instead, aim to understand how your decisions connect. If your infantry lacks rifles, ask why. Maybe you did not assign enough military factories. If your planes do not appear in battle, check whether they are assigned to an air region. Treat the first campaign as a training exercise.

## 2. Start With National Focuses

The national focus tree is one of the first things you should check. A focus usually takes 70 days and gives political, industrial, military, or diplomatic benefits. At the start, industry-focused choices are usually strong because they give extra factories and research bonuses. More factories mean more equipment, and more equipment means stronger armies.

Try to read a few focuses ahead before clicking. Some focuses unlock later options, while others may create diplomatic tension or start conflicts. If you are playing Germany, early industrial and army focuses are useful because they support the country’s natural strategy: building up quickly and preparing for war.

## 3. Build Civilian and Military Factories

Construction is simple once you understand the difference between factory types. Civilian factories build new buildings, trade for resources, and support your economy. Military factories produce weapons, tanks, aircraft, and other equipment. In the early game, building civilian factories first is often a good idea because they improve your long-term construction speed. Later, switch to military factories so your army has enough equipment before the fighting begins.

Do not scatter construction randomly. Build in states with good infrastructure when possible, because construction is faster there. Also remember that an army without equipment is only strong on paper. Divisions need rifles, artillery, support equipment, trucks, tanks, and aircraft depending on their templates.

## 4. Research Practical Technologies

Research slots are valuable. Beginners often make the mistake of researching exciting technology too early, but ahead-of-time penalties can slow progress. Focus on useful basics: industry, electronics, infantry equipment, artillery, and aircraft. Industry technologies improve output and construction, electronics improve research speed, and infantry equipment keeps your army supplied.

For your first game, do not try to research every naval, air, tank, and doctrine path at once. Pick a strategy. If you plan to fight mostly on land, prioritize infantry, artillery, industry, and land doctrine. If you are playing a major naval country, then ships matter more, but even then your economy and army still need attention.

## 5. Understand Production Lines

Production lines turn military factories into equipment. A line becomes more efficient over time, so constantly changing what a factory produces can reduce output. Start with the essentials: infantry equipment, support equipment, artillery, fighters, and trucks. Tanks are powerful but expensive, so beginners should not rely on them before they understand supply and production.

Watch the logistics screen. It shows whether you have a surplus or shortage of each equipment type. If you see a large deficit of infantry equipment, your divisions may not fight effectively. Add more factories to rifles or reduce the number of new divisions you are training. It is better to have fewer fully equipped divisions than a huge army missing basic weapons.

## 6. Organize the Army

Armies in Hearts of Iron IV work best when assigned to generals and field marshals. Select divisions, create an army, assign a general, and draw a front line against a potential enemy. Then create an offensive line to tell the army where it should advance. You can let the battle planner execute the plan, but you should still watch the front and manually adjust when something goes wrong.

Do not attack everywhere at once without preparation. Check terrain, supply, enemy strength, and air support. Mountains, rivers, and forts are difficult to attack. Plains are better for tanks and large offensives. If your attack stalls, stop and reorganize instead of wasting manpower and equipment.

## 7. Pay Attention to Supply

Supply is one of the most important systems for new players to respect. Divisions need food, ammunition, fuel, and equipment to fight. If too many troops are placed in a poor supply area, they will suffer penalties and become easy to defeat. Supply problems often happen in deserts, mountains, forests, and faraway regions with weak infrastructure.

Use the supply map mode when a front feels stuck. If the area is red or orange, reduce the number of divisions there, improve railways, upgrade supply hubs, or use transport planes if available. More divisions are not always better. A smaller force with proper supply can beat a larger force that cannot maintain itself.

## 8. Use Air Power Simply

Air power can decide battles, but it does not need to be complicated at first. Produce fighters and close air support. Fighters help control the skies, while close air support helps your ground troops win battles. Create air wings, assign them to an air base near the front, select an air region, and give them missions.

If your planes are too far from the target region, they will be less effective. If the air base is overcrowded, efficiency drops. Keep air bases reasonably close to the front and avoid placing too many planes in one base.

## 9. Make Limited Goals

The biggest beginner mistake is trying to do everything. Hearts of Iron IV rewards planning. Before a war, decide what you want: capture a specific country, hold a defensive line, secure resources, or support an ally. Your production, research, and army organization should support that goal.

For example, if your goal is to defeat Poland as Germany, you need a strong infantry army, some mobile units, enough fighters, and a clear front line. You do not need a perfect navy or advanced late-game tanks for that first objective. After each success, pause and prepare for the next stage.

## 10. Learn From Defeat

Losing in Hearts of Iron IV is normal. A failed campaign usually teaches more than an easy victory. If you lose, identify one or two reasons instead of blaming the whole game. Did you run out of equipment? Did your troops lack supply? Did the enemy have air superiority? Did you attack through bad terrain? Each answer points to a specific improvement for the next campaign.

The game becomes much easier when you stop seeing it as a single giant system and start seeing it as connected layers. Factories create equipment. Equipment fills divisions. Divisions need supply. Air power supports battles. Research improves all of these systems over time. National focuses guide your country’s development. Once that chain makes sense, the interface becomes less frightening.

## Conclusion

A strong beginner approach is simple: choose a major country, build industry, research practical technology, produce basic equipment, keep divisions supplied, and pursue one goal at a time. You do not need to understand every naval mission, division template, or political trick in your first campaign. Focus on the core loop and let each game teach you one new system.

Hearts of Iron IV is complex, but it is also very readable once you learn what the game is asking from you. Do not rush. Pause often, inspect problems, and make small improvements. After a few campaigns, the map will stop looking like a wall of buttons and start looking like a set of interesting strategic choices.
